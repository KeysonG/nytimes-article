The Smartphone Tech Fast Sweeping College Campuses

Across the country, students are trading their iPhones for flip phones, finding unexpected clarity in disconnection. A growing movement embraces the discomfort of being unreachable.

By Emma Goldberg

hen Maya Chen arrived at Stanford University last fall, she made a decision that baffled her roommates: She left her iPhone at home. In its place, she brought a $30 Nokia flip phone that could do little more than make calls and send texts ‚Äî and even that required pressing the same button three times to type a single letter.

"People thought I was doing some kind of performance art," said Ms. Chen, 20, a junior studying computer science. "But after a semester of doomscrolling and losing hours to TikTok, I needed something drastic."

She is part of a growing movement of college students who are experimenting with what they call "tech fasts" ‚Äî deliberate periods of disconnection from smartphones and social media that range from a few hours to entire semesters.

"My brain felt like it was constantly buffering. I couldn't focus on anything for more than five minutes."

At universities across the country, students are forming clubs dedicated to phone-free activities, organizing "dumbphone" swaps where they trade smartphones for basic devices, and even creating designated "no-scroll zones" in libraries and dining halls.

A Return to Simpler Times

The flip phone revival began as a trickle but has become something closer to a stream. Sales of "dumbphones" ‚Äî basic phones without app stores or internet browsers ‚Äî increased 35 percent in 2025, according to market research firm Counterpoint.

For many students, the appeal goes beyond productivity. They describe experiencing something that feels almost novel: boredom.

"The first few days without my phone, I didn't know what to do with myself," said Jake Morrison, 21, a senior at the University of Texas at Austin. "I was standing in line for coffee and I just... stood there. No scrolling. It felt bizarre."

The Science of Disconnection

Research supports what these students are feeling. Studies have linked excessive smartphone use to increased rates of anxiety, depression and attention problems, particularly among young people.

'I Feel Like Myself Again'

But for students like Ms. Chen, the benefits of disconnection have been profound. Her grades improved. She started reading books again ‚Äî actual physical books. She joined an intramural soccer team.

"I know it sounds dramatic, but I feel like myself again," she said. "I didn't realize how much of my identity had become wrapped up in this little rectangle."

She acknowledges the irony of studying computer science while eschewing a smartphone. But she sees no contradiction.

"I'm learning to build technology," she said. "That means I should understand how it affects us ‚Äî the good and the bad."

"I'm not anti-phone," she said. "I'm just pro-presence."
